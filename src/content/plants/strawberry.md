Strawberries combine high grocery value ($5–$8/lb organic), perennial productivity, and the irresistible appeal of fresh-picked berries. While the first year is modest, established strawberry beds compound in value as runner plants fill in — eventually yielding dozens of free plants.

## Growing Tips

Plant strawberries from bare-root crowns or transplants in early spring (or fall in warm climates). Space June-bearing types 18 inches apart; everbearing types can be 12 inches.

### Types to Know
- **June-bearing** — One large crop in early summer; highest individual yields; best for preserving
- **Everbearing** — Two smaller crops (spring and fall); more consistent harvest; better for fresh eating
- **Day-neutral** — Produce continuously through the season regardless of day length; excellent for containers

### First-Year Flowers
For June-bearing types, **pinch off all flowers the first year**. This redirects energy to root development and dramatically increases second-year yields. Everbearing types can be allowed to fruit after the first flush of flowers.

### Runners
Strawberry plants send out runners (stolons) that create new daughter plants. Allow some to root to expand your bed; remove others to focus energy on fruit. After 3–4 years, most home plantings benefit from renovation.

## Bed Renovation
Every 3–4 years, renovate June-bearing beds right after harvest: mow leaves to 1 inch, thin plants to 5–6 inches apart, fertilize, and water well. This rejuvenates the bed for several more productive years.

## Mulching
Straw mulch (hence the name) protects fruit from soil contact and rot, retains moisture, and keeps roots cool. In cold climates, thick straw mulch protects crowns over winter.

## Pest & Disease Watch
- **Slugs & snails:** Attracted to berries; use iron phosphate bait
- **Birds:** Use bird netting once fruits begin to color
- **Gray mold (Botrytis):** Remove infected berries promptly; improve air circulation

## Harvest
Pick berries when fully red with no white or green tips. They don't ripen further once picked. Harvest every 2–3 days at peak season. Handle gently — fresh strawberries bruise easily.

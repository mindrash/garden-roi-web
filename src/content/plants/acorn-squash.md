Acorn squash is one of the most reliable and productive winter squash for home gardens. Unlike butternut, which requires a long growing season, acorn squash matures in 80-100 days - manageable from zone 4 onward. Unlike spaghetti squash, which has modest flavor, acorn squash has a genuinely rich, slightly sweet flesh that works well with simple preparations. Each plant produces 2-4 squash at $2-4 retail value per fruit.

The storage is the secondary value. Properly cured acorn squash keeps 1-3 months, extending the garden's productive value into winter. A 4-plant planting producing 10-15 squash in September provides vegetables through November or December.

## What it actually is

*Cucurbita pepo* is the same species as zucchini and spaghetti squash - one of the most cultivated vegetable species globally. Acorn squash is characterized by its acorn shape (pointed at the blossom end, ribbed), dark green to golden exterior, and orange-yellow flesh. It's smaller and more manageable than butternut or hubbard squash - 1-2 lb per fruit.

The flavor is sweeter and more complex than spaghetti squash, with a nuttiness that takes well to brown butter, maple, and spices.

**Varieties:**

- **Table Queen (Table Ace):** the classic dark green acorn. Reliable, compact vine, 2-3 lb fruits. The standard.
- **Carnival:** mottled yellow, orange, and green skin; slightly sweeter than Table Queen. Shorter storage life.
- **Delicata:** technically a delicata squash (same species), not a true acorn, but similar size and management. Cream-colored with green stripes; excellent sweet flavor. 75-90 days.
- **Sweet Dumpling:** very small (0.5-1 lb), striped, individual-serving size. Good for single-serving roasting.
- **Honey Bear:** All-America Selection winner. More compact vine than most acorn varieties; improved disease resistance. Good for small gardens.

## Growing requirements

**Timing:** same as other winter squash - direct sow after soil reaches 65°F. Acorn squash's shorter season (80-100 days) gives it more flexibility than butternut or Hubbard. In zone 4-5, this means direct sowing in early June for a late-September harvest; in zones 6-9, late May.

**Space:** standard acorn squash vines reach 6-8 feet. Hill planting (3 seeds per hill, thin to 2 plants) at 4-foot centers gives each plant room. Compact varieties (Honey Bear, bush Delicata) need only 3-4 feet.

**Soil:** fertile, well-drained. Amend planting hills with compost before planting. Side-dress with balanced fertilizer when vines are 2-3 feet long; again at fruit set.

**Watering:** consistent moisture at the root zone. The critical period is fruit set and early development - irregular moisture during this phase causes fruit abortion or blossom end rot. Drip irrigation reduces fungal disease pressure that overhead watering creates.

**Pollination:** acorn squash produces separate male and female flowers. Males appear first. Female flowers have a tiny immature squash at the base of the flower. Bees handle pollination; hand-pollinate if bee activity is low or if early fruit set fails.

## What goes wrong

**Powdery mildew** appears on all winter squash by late summer. Less problematic for acorn squash than for butternut because acorn squash is harvested earlier. Still worth treating to prevent premature defoliation: potassium bicarbonate spray, remove heavily affected leaves, ensure good air circulation.

**Squash vine borer** is more damaging in eastern North America. Row cover until flowering prevents egg-laying; after removing cover for pollination, inspect the base of vines weekly. Inject Bt (*Bacillus thuringiensis* var. *kurstaki*) into borer entry holes with a syringe; mound soil over the injection point so the vine can re-root above the damage.

**Squash bugs** (*Anasa tristis*): remove egg masses from under leaves before they hatch. Nymphs are easier to kill than adults; Neem oil spray targets young nymphs.

**Waterlogged soil** causes root rot and vine collapse. Raised hills help drainage; avoid planting in low spots.

**Sunken spots on fruit (blossom end rot):** calcium deficiency or irregular watering causes this. Consistent watering is the primary prevention.

## Harvest and storage

Harvest when skin is hard, deep-colored (green varieties are fully dark green; multicolor varieties show full color development), and the skin resists fingernail pressure. The stem should be fully corky and dry. Leave 2-3 inches of stem attached. Don't harvest early - acorn squash flavor and storage life both improve with full maturity.

**Curing:** 10-14 days at 70-80°F in a well-ventilated location. Unlike butternut and Hubbard, acorn squash doesn't cure as dramatically - it benefits mostly from surface drying. Don't cure at temperatures below 55°F.

**Storage:** 1-3 months at 50-55°F. Shorter storage than butternut (which keeps 3-6 months); plan accordingly. Use acorn squash first in your winter storage lineup.

**Preparing:** the small size makes acorn squash the most practical of the winter squash for single-serving preparations. Standard method: halve, scoop seeds, oil and season the cut face, roast cut-side-down at 400°F for 35-45 minutes until tender. The seeds can be cleaned and roasted like pumpkin seeds.

**Core preparations:**

- **Roasted with brown butter and maple syrup:** the classic American preparation. Halve, roast, then fill the cavity with brown butter, maple syrup, and salt. The flesh sweetens as it roasts; the butter and maple amplify it.

- **Stuffed acorn squash:** the cavity is a natural bowl. Fill with wild rice and mushrooms, sausage and sage, or quinoa and roasted vegetables. Bake stuffed until everything is tender.

- **Acorn squash soup:** roast, scoop, blend with vegetable or chicken broth, cream, and spices (ginger, nutmeg, white pepper). The flavor is sweeter and more nuanced than butternut squash soup.

- **Mashed:** scoop roasted flesh, mash with butter and a small amount of cream. Works well alongside roast chicken or pork.

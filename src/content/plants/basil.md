Basil is the ultimate high-value herb. At $4–6 for a small store bunch that weighs barely 1 oz, growing your own can save $100+ per season. It also improves the flavor and growth of nearby tomatoes — making it a perfect companion.

## Growing Tips

Start seeds indoors 6 weeks before the last frost, or direct sow after soil warms. Basil hates cold and will turn black below 50°F — protect transplants until nights are reliably warm.

### Pinching for Productivity
The most important technique: **pinch the top set of leaves as soon as the plant has 3–4 sets of leaves**. This forces lateral branching and turns one stem into a bushy, productive plant. Pinch any time you see the center trying to bolt (send up a flower stalk).

### Soil
Prefers well-draining, fertile soil with a pH around 6.0–7.0. Add compost at planting. Basil is a moderate feeder — a balanced liquid fertilizer once a month is enough once established.

### Watering
Keep soil consistently moist but not waterlogged. Water at the base, not on leaves, to prevent fungal issues. Basil wilts dramatically when dry but usually recovers quickly after watering.

## Bolting
When basil flowers, leaf production slows and flavor becomes bitter. Pinch flower heads off as soon as they appear to extend harvest. Eventually every plant will bolt — at that point, harvest the remaining leaves and let it set seed for next year.

## Varieties Worth Growing
- **Genovese Basil** — Classic Italian, large leaves, best for pesto
- **Thai Basil** — More pungent, holds heat better in cooking, beautiful purple flowers
- **Lemon Basil** — Citrusy, great in fish dishes and cocktails
- **Purple Basil** — Decorative, slightly less intense flavor

## Harvest & Preservation
Harvest in the morning after dew dries. Make pesto, dry in a single layer, blend with olive oil and freeze in ice cube trays, or pack whole leaves in salt for long-term storage.

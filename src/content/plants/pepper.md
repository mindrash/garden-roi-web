Bell peppers are slow but rewarding. A single plant can produce 8–10 large peppers per season — worth $20–$30 at grocery prices, especially organic. They thrive with warmth, so patience early in the season pays off.

## Growing Tips

Peppers need a long growing season — start seeds indoors **10–12 weeks before last frost**, or buy transplants. They require warm soil (65°F minimum) to thrive.

### Temperature Management
Peppers won't set fruit when nighttime temps drop below 55°F or daytime highs exceed 90°F. In extreme heat, blossoms drop — shade cloth (30–40% shade) can help in hot climates. In cool climates, row cover early in the season dramatically boosts yields.

### Fertilizing
Feed with a balanced fertilizer at planting. Once flowering begins, switch to a phosphorus-heavy fertilizer to support fruit development. Too much nitrogen = all leaves, no peppers.

### Support
Mature pepper plants get heavy and can snap in wind. Stake plants or use small tomato cages early in the season.

## Color Changes
Green peppers are simply immature red (or yellow/orange) peppers. Leaving them on the plant longer to fully ripen increases sweetness and vitamin C content but reduces overall plant yield. Harvest some green and let some fully ripen for a balance of productivity and flavor.

## Pest & Disease Watch
- **Pepper weevils:** Small beetles that bore into peppers. Remove affected fruits immediately.
- **Aphids & spider mites:** Common in hot, dry conditions. Blast with water or use insecticidal soap.
- **Bacterial spot:** Dark lesions on leaves; caused by wet conditions. Improve air circulation.

## Harvest
Clip peppers from the plant (don't pull — you can break branches). Fully colored peppers store well in the refrigerator for 1–2 weeks, or can be roasted and frozen.

Chickpeas grown fresh and eaten green from the pod are a different vegetable than the dried chickpea in a can. At the fresh stage — harvested before the pods dry down — they taste like a cross between a pea and a green bean, with a nutty richness that the dried version approximates but can't replicate. Fresh green chickpeas sell at $5-8/lb at specialty and Indian grocery stores, where they're sold as *chhole* or *hara chana*. The dried version — *kabuli chana* or garbanzo — retails at $2-5/lb.

Growing your own gives you both options and one you can't easily find commercially: roasted fresh chickpeas, eaten pod and all like edamame, which is arguably the best way to eat them.

## What it actually is

*Cicer arietinum* is an annual cool-season legume in the family Fabaceae, one of the oldest cultivated crops — archaeological evidence places it in cultivation in the Middle East and India 7,500 years ago (Zohary et al., *Domestication of Plants in the Old World*, 4th ed., 2012). It grows 1-2 feet tall as a bushy plant with small, compound leaves covered in fine hairs that secrete a mild malic acid (you can lick the leaf — it tastes slightly sour).

Two market types:

**Desi chickpea**: small, darker-colored, angular seeds. Higher fiber content, stronger flavor, primarily grown in India and Ethiopia. Lower yield in garden settings.

**Kabuli chickpea**: large, round, cream-colored — the type in canned chickpeas. Milder flavor, higher yield. Most seed catalog varieties are Kabuli types.

| Type | Seed size | Color | Flavor | Market use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Kabuli | Large | Cream/tan | Mild, nutty | Canned, hummus, Western cooking |
| Desi | Small | Brown, black, speckled | Stronger, earthier | Indian cooking, flour (besan) |

Chickpea is a cool-season crop — it grows best at 60-75°F air temperatures. Unlike most legumes, it tolerates light frost and can be planted earlier in spring than beans. In hot-summer climates, it's a spring-only or fall crop; in mild coastal climates (zones 9-10), it's a winter crop.

## The ROI case

Chickpeas have lower per-plant yield than many vegetables, but they also have the lowest input requirements. No spraying, minimal fertilizing (nitrogen-fixer), drought-tolerant once established.

A 10-foot row at 4-inch spacing (30 plants) produces approximately 0.5-0.75 lb of dried shelled chickpeas, or 1.0-1.5 lb of fresh green pods.

| Harvest mode | Yield (10 ft row) | Value | Seed cost | Net |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dried (mature) | 0.5-0.75 lb | $2.00-3.75 | $0.75* | $1.25-3.00 |

The fresh green harvest is substantially more valuable than the dried. If you're growing in a short-season climate where drying down fully is uncertain, harvesting green at 60-70 days (before the pods brown) captures the peak value.

## Growing requirements

**Temperature:** chickpeas are cool-season but not cold-season. They germinate at 60°F soil temperature, tolerate light frost (25-28°F) on established plants, but stop growing above 85°F and drop flowers in sustained heat. In most of the US: plant 4-6 weeks before last spring frost for a spring crop; plant in late summer for a fall crop in zones 7+.

**Direct sowing:** sow 1-2 inches deep, 4-6 inches apart. Germination in 7-14 days at 60-70°F. Don't crowd — chickpeas need airflow to prevent fungal issues.

**Soil:** well-drained, moderately fertile. Chickpeas do poorly in heavy, wet soils. Sandy loam or loamy soil is ideal. Like other legumes, they don't need nitrogen fertilizer if inoculated with *Mesorhizobium ciceri* inoculant (chickpea-specific; general legume inoculant doesn't always carry the right strain).

**Water:** established plants are quite drought-tolerant. Too much moisture invites botrytis and other fungal diseases. Water at the base; avoid wetting foliage. Reduce irrigation once pods begin to form and you're targeting dried harvest.

## What goes wrong

**Botrytis (grey mold)** is the primary disease problem in wet, cool, humid conditions. It appears as fuzzy gray growth on stems and pods. Prevention: adequate spacing, base watering, good airflow. Remove and dispose of infected material immediately.

**Ascochyta blight** (*Ascochyta rabiei*) causes lesions on stems, leaves, and pods that can destroy an entire planting. It spreads rapidly in wet weather. Practice a 3-year rotation — don't grow chickpeas in the same bed more than once every 3 years. Use pathogen-free seed (buy from reputable suppliers, not saved seed from infected crops).

**Aphids** cluster on tender growing tips. A strong water spray removes most of them. Chickpeas' acidic leaf secretions deter many pests — the aphid issue is usually minor.

**Seed not germinating:** chickpea seed is large and has a hard coat. Poor germination usually means cold soil (below 55°F) or very old seed. Pre-soak 8 hours before planting to speed germination in marginal conditions.

## Harvest and use

**Fresh green harvest (hara chana):** at 60-75 days, when pods are plump and green. Pods should feel full but the plants should still look green — don't wait until they start yellowing. The whole immature pod is edible when harvested young enough; older green pods require shelling.

**Dried harvest:** allow pods to brown and dry on the plant completely. The plants die back, pods rattle when shaken. Harvest the whole plant, hang to finish drying 1-2 weeks, then shell. Store dried chickpeas in airtight containers; they keep 2-3 years.

**Roasting fresh:** toss fresh green pods in olive oil and coarse salt. Roast at 400°F for 12-15 minutes until pods are charred in spots and the chickpeas inside are tender. Eat pod and all, like edamame. The flavor is nutty, slightly sweet, with a charred edge.

**Hummus from fresh-dried homegrown:** cook dried chickpeas (soak overnight, then simmer 1-2 hours until very soft). The homegrown version, used fresh rather than sitting in storage for months, has better flavor and cooks more evenly. Standard hummus: 2 cups cooked chickpeas, 3 tablespoons tahini, 2 tablespoons lemon juice, 1 garlic clove, 3-4 tablespoons olive oil, salt. Process until smooth.

**Besan (chickpea flour):** dry chickpeas ground to a fine flour. Used in Indian cooking for pakoras, besan ladoo, and as a binding agent. Grind in a powerful blender or grain mill. Desi type chickpeas (darker, stronger flavor) produce the traditional besan flavor; Kabuli type produces a milder flour.

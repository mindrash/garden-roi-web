Green beans are among the easiest, most productive beginner vegetables. Direct sow seeds — no transplants needed — and harvest in as little as 50 days. Bush beans need no support and produce a concentrated crop; pole beans offer continuous production all season from a small footprint.

## Growing Tips

Direct sow seeds 1 inch deep after the last frost when soil temperature is at least 60°F. Beans don't like transplanting. No pre-soaking necessary, though soaking for 8 hours speeds germination.

### Bush vs. Pole
- **Bush beans:** Compact (18 inches tall), no support needed, produce a single heavy crop over 2–3 weeks. Great for canning when you want a lot at once.
- **Pole beans:** Climb to 6–8 feet, need a trellis or poles, produce continuously for months. Better overall yield per square foot; better for fresh eating.

### Soil & Fertilizing
Beans fix their own nitrogen from the air via root bacteria — **do not over-fertilize with nitrogen**. Too much nitrogen produces lush plants with poor pod production. A low-nitrogen fertilizer at planting is sufficient; established plants need little additional feeding.

## Succession Planting (Bush Beans)
To avoid a single overwhelming harvest, sow a new planting of bush beans every 2–3 weeks through midsummer. Stop sowing 8–10 weeks before your first fall frost.

## Harvest
Harvest when pods are pencil-thin and snap cleanly. If seeds start to bulge through the pod walls, beans are past peak. Pick every 2–3 days — leaving mature pods on the plant signals it to stop producing.

## Preservation
- **Fresh:** Refrigerate unwashed in a bag up to a week
- **Blanch & freeze:** Blanch 3 minutes, cool in ice water, freeze — preserves flavor and color for 8–12 months
- **Pickling:** Quick-pickle with dill and garlic for "dilly beans"

## Pest Watch
- **Mexican bean beetle:** Yellow-orange beetles that skeletonize leaves. Hand-pick egg clusters from undersides of leaves.
- **Bean leaf beetles:** Small, round beetles. Row cover early in season provides protection.

Zucchini is famously — sometimes infamously — productive. Two plants are genuinely enough for a family of four; three will have you leaving bags on neighbors' doorsteps. With virtually no pest pressure when young and quick days to harvest, it's one of the most reliable high-yield vegetables in any garden.

## Growing Tips

Direct sow seeds or transplant seedlings after the last frost, when soil is warm (65°F+). Plant in hills of 2–3 seedlings, spaced 3–4 feet apart. Zucchini plants get large — give them room.

### Space Management
One zucchini plant needs about 4 square feet of space (some sprawl to 6'+ wide). If space is tight, train the main vine in one direction, or grow compact bush varieties like 'Patio Star' or 'Round Robin' in containers.

### Pollination
Like cucumbers, zucchini has separate male and female flowers. Large numbers of male flowers appear first. The plant isn't failing — it needs bees to pollinate once female flowers (with tiny zucchini at the base) appear. If fruit shrivels and drops off, hand-pollinate using a cotton swab or small brush.

## Common Disease: Powdery Mildew
Almost every zucchini plant gets powdery mildew (white coating on leaves) by midsummer. It's inevitable but manageable:
- Don't panic — light mildew doesn't significantly reduce yields
- Remove badly affected leaves
- Preventively spray with diluted baking soda solution or neem oil

## Harvest
**This is critical:** Harvest zucchini when they're 6–8 inches long. Fruits left on the plant grow into marrows overnight and divert energy from new fruit production. Check plants daily during peak season. Overgrown zucchini can still be used — scoop out seeds and stuff them; shred for zucchini bread.

## Varieties
- **Black Beauty** — Classic dark green, high yield, reliable
- **Yellow Crookneck / Straightneck** — Yellow summer squash; same care, mild flavor
- **Patio Star** — Compact bush type for small spaces and containers
- **Tromboncino** — Italian heirloom; vine type, nutty flavor, pest-resistant

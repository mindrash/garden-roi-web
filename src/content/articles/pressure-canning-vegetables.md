The water bath canner has one job: it kills organisms that cause spoilage and illness in high-acid foods. Boiling water reaches 212°F at sea level. That's enough to destroy the vegetative forms of bacteria, yeasts, and molds that would otherwise ruin your tomatoes, jams, and pickles.

It is not enough for low-acid vegetables.

*Clostridium botulinum* produces spores that survive boiling water indefinitely. In a sealed, low-oxygen, low-acid environment - which is exactly what a sealed canning jar provides - those spores germinate and the bacteria produces botulinum toxin. Botulism is rare, but it is a serious illness and it is specifically associated with improperly canned low-acid foods. The destruction temperature for botulinum spores is 240°F, which water cannot reach at normal pressure. Pressure canning reaches 240°F by raising the boiling point of water under pressure. That is the entire reason pressure canning exists.

This is not overcaution. The National Center for Home Food Preservation (NCHFP), the USDA's authoritative source on home canning safety, is explicit: all low-acid foods - vegetables, meats, poultry, fish, and combinations containing these - must be processed in a pressure canner. Water bath processing of these foods is not a shortcut. It does not work. The risk is specific and real (NCHFP, *Complete Guide to Home Canning*, USDA Agriculture Information Bulletin No. 539, 2015, revised 2021).

If you have an older canning book published before about 1990, or a recipe from a family member that uses water bath for green beans, set it aside. Botulism from home canning is uncommon in part because home canning itself has declined, and in part because the instructions have improved. Earlier guidance was less conservative. Don't use it.

## Which Vegetables Require Pressure Canning

Low-acid vegetables are those with a pH above 4.6. All of these require pressure canning:

| Vegetable | pH range | Typical dial-gauge pressure | Processing time (pints) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Green beans | 5.3-5.7 | 11 lbs (0-2,000 ft) | 20 minutes |
| Corn (whole kernel) | 5.9-6.5 | 11 lbs (0-2,000 ft) | 55 minutes |
| Beets | 5.5-6.6 | 11 lbs (0-2,000 ft) | 30 minutes |
| Carrots | 5.0-6.0 | 11 lbs (0-2,000 ft) | 25 minutes |
| Potatoes (cubed) | 5.6-6.0 | 11 lbs (0-2,000 ft) | 35 minutes |
| Asparagus | 5.7-6.1 | 11 lbs (0-2,000 ft) | 30 minutes |
| Peas (shelled) | 5.8-6.4 | 11 lbs (0-2,000 ft) | 40 minutes |

Processing times and pressures are for dial-gauge canners at 0-2,000 feet elevation. Processing times listed are minimums established through USDA testing. Do not shorten them.

Water bath canning is appropriate for high-acid foods: tomatoes (with added acid), all fruits, pickles (acidity from vinegar), and jams and jellies. For a full comparison of which foods use which method, see the [water bath vs. pressure canning guide](/homestead/water-bath-vs-pressure-canning/).

## Equipment: Dial-Gauge vs. Weighted-Gauge

Two types of pressure canners dominate the market. The choice affects both cost and long-term maintenance.

**Dial-gauge canners (Presto, Mirro):** use a pressure gauge that shows exact PSI. Price range: $80-130 for a 16-quart model. The gauge tells you precisely what pressure you're maintaining. The downside: dial gauges drift over time and must be tested annually at a county extension office (most do this free or for a small fee). An untested gauge that reads 11 PSI when the actual pressure is 8 PSI is dangerous. If you don't have access to testing, or can't remember to get it done annually, this is a liability.

**Weighted-gauge canners (All American):** use a jiggling weight that audibly signals when pressure is correct - it jiggles 1-3 times per minute at the correct pressure. No gauge to calibrate. All American canners cost $200-350 for a 15-quart model because they're machined aluminum with metal-to-metal seals (no rubber gasket to replace). They're expensive upfront and maintenance-free long-term. Many All American canners have been in continuous use for 40+ years.

For most home canners who will use the equipment a few times per year, the Presto is reasonable and the annual gauge check is a manageable maintenance task. For serious canners who process 50-100+ jars per season, the All American's durability and zero-maintenance gauge design makes the higher price rational.

| Feature | Dial-gauge (Presto) | Weighted-gauge (All American) |
|---|---|---|
| Price | $80-130 | $200-350 |
| Gauge accuracy | Drifts; needs annual testing | Fixed weights; no drift |
| Maintenance | Annual gauge check | Replace gasket periodically (if applicable) |
| Seal type | Rubber gasket | Metal-to-metal |
| Longevity | 15-20 years typical | 40+ years with care |

## Altitude Adjustment

At higher elevations, water boils at lower temperatures because atmospheric pressure is lower. The pressure settings used at sea level don't achieve 240°F at elevation. If you're above 1,000 feet, you must increase the processing pressure to compensate.

**Dial-gauge canners - altitude adjustment:**

| Elevation | Pressure (PSI) |
|---|---|
| 0-2,000 ft | 11 PSI |
| 2,001-4,000 ft | 12 PSI |
| 4,001-6,000 ft | 13 PSI |
| 6,001-8,000 ft | 14 PSI |

**Weighted-gauge canners:** use the 10-lb weight below 1,000 feet; the 15-lb weight at 1,000 feet and above. No intermediate settings are available, which is why weighted gauges are slightly less precise but also impossible to miscalibrate.

Denver, Colorado sits at 5,280 feet - well within the zone requiring 13 PSI on a dial-gauge canner. Salt Lake City is at 4,226 feet. Albuquerque is at 5,312 feet. If you're in an interior western city and you're processing at sea-level pressure, you are not reaching 240°F and your low-acid canned goods are not safe.

## The Process

Pressure canning follows a specific sequence. Every step exists for a reason.

**Preparation:** wash all equipment. Inspect jars for cracks or chips on the rim (discard damaged jars). Use new lids each time; bands can be reused if they're not rusty or bent. Jars should be clean and hot when filled.

**Headspace:** most vegetables require 1-inch headspace. This is the gap between the food surface and the top of the jar. The headspace allows for food expansion during processing and is necessary for the lid to seal. Vegetables that expand significantly during processing (corn, peas) need the full inch. Don't overfill.

**Venting the canner:** before putting the weight or closing the petcock, allow the canner to vent steam steadily for 10 minutes. This removes air from the canner. Air in the canner causes uneven heat distribution; the canner will show correct pressure on the gauge while actual temperatures are lower than expected. The 10-minute vent step is non-negotiable.

**Processing:** bring pressure up to the correct level, then begin timing. Adjust heat to maintain steady pressure throughout. Pressure fluctuations - dropping and then surging back up - extend total processing time and stress jar seals. After processing is complete, turn off heat and allow the canner to depressurize naturally. Do not use cold water to speed cooling. Do not attempt to open the canner until pressure has returned to zero.

## Cost Per Jar: The Real Math

The economics of pressure canning depend on how you account for equipment costs and how many jars you process over the life of the canner.

**Assumptions for this calculation:**
- Presto 23-quart canner: $110
- Amortized over 10 years, 20 canning sessions per year = 200 sessions
- Equipment cost per session: $0.55
- Typical session produces 7 quarts = $0.08 equipment cost per jar
- New lid per jar: $0.25
- Energy (propane or electric, 90 minutes total): approximately $0.15-0.25
- Your labor (not included - depends on your time valuation)

**True cost per jar (pressure canned vegetables):** approximately $0.48-0.58 per jar, plus the food itself.

**Comparison values:**
- Store-bought canned green beans, 14.5 oz can: $0.80-1.20
- Store-bought canned corn: $0.90-1.30
- Organic canned green beans: $1.80-2.50

Home-canned green beans from your own harvest: food cost is essentially zero (seed + water + time growing). The jar cost of $0.50 per pint compares favorably to $0.80-1.20 for a roughly equivalent can of commercial beans.

The math improves significantly if you grow the vegetables yourself. Canning produce you purchased to can often doesn't pencil out unless you're buying in bulk at peak-season prices. If your [green bean](/crops/green-bean/) row produced 20 lb of surplus beans in August that would otherwise go to waste, canning them converts that surplus into 20+ pints of vegetables worth $16-20 at grocery store prices.

## Best Crops for Pressure Canning ROI

Not all vegetables are worth the effort.

**Green beans** are the best ROI pressure canning crop by most measures. They're easy to grow in large quantities, they can in pints and quarts quickly (20 minutes for pints), and home-canned green beans are genuinely better than commercial canned beans - firmer texture, no can-lining flavor, controlled salt content. A [green bean](/crops/green-bean/) planting of 30 row feet producing 20 lb surplus: converted to pints at 1 lb per pint = 20 pints of beans, retail value $16-20.

**Corn** is more time-intensive (shucking, cutting from cob, processing at 55 minutes for pints) but produces a product with no commercial equivalent. Home-canned corn has noticeably better flavor than commercial canned corn because you can start with fresh-picked ears processed within hours of harvest.

**Tomatoes** are mostly water-bath crops, not pressure canning crops - the natural acidity handles them. Tomato-based mixtures with added low-acid vegetables (tomato-pepper salsa, tomato-vegetable soup mixtures) require pressure canning if the recipe isn't an established tested USDA recipe. Don't improvise on mixed tomato products. See the [canning financial case](/homestead/canning-financial-case/) for tomato-specific economics.

## What You Cannot Pressure Can at Home

Some products cannot be safely processed in a home pressure canner regardless of pressure or time:

**Butter and dairy products:** the fat and protein matrix prevents consistent heat penetration. Botulinum toxin can survive in localized unheated pockets. No tested home processing time exists.

**Pasta, rice, and bread:** starch products expand and absorb moisture inconsistently, preventing reliable heat penetration to the center of each jar.

**Flour-thickened products:** gravies, cream soups, and stews thickened with flour or cornstarch. The thick matrix prevents heat penetration. NCHFP has tested and does not recommend home canning of thick, starch-based products.

**Mashed or pureed vegetables:** the dense consistency prevents uniform heat penetration. Chunk-style potato pieces can be pressure canned; mashed potatoes cannot.

These prohibitions reflect tested science, not caution. If you see a recipe online for home-canned butter or canned mashed potatoes, it is not a tested safe recipe regardless of how it's presented. The NCHFP maintains a database of tested home canning recipes at nchfp.uga.edu. Use tested recipes. When in doubt, freeze instead.

Every spring, gardeners spend hundreds of dollars on seeds, amendments, tools, and transplants with a vague sense that it'll "pay off somehow." But does it? The answer depends entirely on what you grow, how much you harvest, and what you'd pay at the store.

Garden ROI — return on investment — is simply the grocery value of what you harvested minus what you spent to grow it.

$$\text{Garden ROI} = \text{Harvest Value} - \text{Total Expenses}$$

If that number is positive, your garden paid for itself and then some. If it's negative, you're essentially paying a premium for the gardening experience — which is fine, but worth knowing.

## Why Most Gardeners Never Break Even (At First)

The first year is always the hardest financially. You're buying tools, building beds, amending soil, and making expensive mistakes. Tools are a one-time cost; beds and soil improvements persist for years.

Year 1 is an investment. Years 2–5 are where the real ROI compounds.

## Which Crops Have the Best ROI?

Not all crops are created equal. The formula for high-ROI crops is:

**High grocery price per pound + Easy to grow + High yield per square foot**

| Crop | Avg Grocery Price | Ease | ROI Tier |
|------|------------------|------|----------|
| Fresh herbs (basil, cilantro) | $10–$20/lb | Easy | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
| Salad greens | $6–$12/lb | Easy | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
| Tomatoes | $2–$4/lb | Medium | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
| Peppers | $3–$5/lb | Medium | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| Cucumbers | $1.50–$2/lb | Easy | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| Zucchini | $1.50–$2/lb | Very Easy | ⭐⭐ |
| Potatoes | $0.80–$1.20/lb | Easy | ⭐⭐ |

**The herb rule:** Fresh herbs are obscenely expensive per pound at the grocery store and obscenely easy to grow in small spaces. A pot of basil worth $4 at the store costs $0.50 in seeds and produces 10x that weight over a season.

## Tracking Your Real Numbers

Theoretical ROI is easy. Tracking *actual* ROI means recording:

1. **Every harvest** — weight or estimated value per harvest event
2. **Every expense** — seeds, transplants, supplies, soil amendments, tools (prorate long-lasting tools over several years)
3. **Break-even date** — the day your harvest value surpasses your expenses

## The Grocery Tier Factor

Your actual savings depend on which grocery tier you shop. If you buy organic produce at a premium grocery store, your ROI is dramatically higher than if you buy conventional produce at a discount store.

- **Budget grocery shoppers:** Save ~$0.70 per dollar of grocery-equivalent yield
- **Standard grocery shoppers:** Break even ~1:1
- **Organic shoppers:** Save ~$1.50+ per dollar of yield

This is why two identical gardens can have completely different ROI profiles for different households.

## The Goal: Breaking Even by August

A well-planned, medium-scale home vegetable garden (4–6 raised beds or ~200 sq ft) should be able to break even on annual operating costs (seeds, transplants, soil amendments, water) by mid-August in a normal season.

Your first-year break-even may extend to October or not happen at all. That's normal. By Year 3, most gardeners regularly break even by July and end the season $200–$400 ahead.

## Getting Started

The fastest path to positive ROI:
1. **Start with herbs** — basil, cilantro, parsley. Tiny space, huge grocery value.
2. **Add salad greens** — lettuce, arugula, spinach. Fast-growing, continuous harvest.
3. **Then tomatoes** — highest total yield value in most gardens.
4. **Track everything** — you can't improve what you don't measure.

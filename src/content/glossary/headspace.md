Headspace is the gap between the surface of food (or liquid) in a canning jar and the underside of the lid. It is measured from the top of the jar down to the food or liquid surface.

This seems like a minor detail. It isn't. Incorrect headspace is one of the most common causes of seal failure in home canning, and seal failure defeats the entire preservation purpose.

## Why Headspace Matters

During heat processing, the food and liquid inside the jar expand and release trapped air. This expansion pushes air up and out of the jar through the lid (which is designed to allow this). When the jar cools after processing, the contents contract and the space inside the jar is at lower pressure than the outside air, pulling the lid down and creating the vacuum seal.

**Too little headspace:** The expanding food or liquid may push against the lid before all trapped air has vented, carrying food particles into the sealing compound. Food debris in the seal creates a pathway for air and microorganisms. The seal may fail immediately or may appear sealed but fail during storage.

**Too much headspace:** Not enough air is expelled during processing. The resulting vacuum is weaker than needed for a reliable seal. The excess air left in the jar also means more oxidation of the contents during storage, affecting quality and color.

## Standard Headspace Measurements

The National Center for Home Food Preservation (NCHFP) specifies headspace for every tested recipe. The general guidelines:

| Food type | Headspace |
|-----------|-----------|
| Jams, jellies, preserves | 1/4 inch |
| Fruits, tomatoes (water bath) | 1/2 inch |
| Pickles, relishes | 1/2 inch |
| Low-acid vegetables (pressure canning) | 1 inch |
| Meats (pressure canned) | 1-1.5 inches |

These are not guidelines to deviate from. NCHFP recipes are tested to achieve specific outcomes assuming correct headspace; departing from these measurements alters processing outcomes in ways that have not been tested for safety.

## Measuring Headspace

Use a canning funnel (centers the jar under the fill point and reduces drips on the rim) and a headspace measuring tool or ruler. Many canning funnels have headspace markings built into the side. Accurate measurement at the point of filling - not eyeballed from above - is what the tested recipes require.

After filling, wipe jar rims with a clean, damp cloth to remove any food residue before placing lids. Food on the rim can interfere with the sealing compound.

## Checking Seals After Processing

After jars cool for 12-24 hours, check that lids have sealed by pressing the center of each lid - it should be firm and not flex up and down. A sealed lid does not move; an unsealed lid flexes. Also: sealed lids make no sound when tapped; unsealed lids sound hollow.

Refrigerate and use within 2-4 days any jars that don't seal. Don't reprocess unless done immediately after processing failure; the food quality after two heat-processing events is usually poor.
